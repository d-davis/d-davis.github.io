Living with change: An archaeological study of human settlement patterns as environmental adaptations in Late Holocene Madagascar

General overview of dissertation project and its broader merits for anthropology

People on Madagascar have coped with environmental change for millennia. Present-day environmental change, however, driven in large part by significant shifts in climate, is negatively impacting the livelihoods and sustainability of coastal communities on Madagascar. As a result of increasing climate-driven impacts on livelihoods, community mobility and settlement strategies are shifting. This project will investigate settlement choice and community mobility in coastal foraging populations using Late Holocene Madagascar as a case study. This will provide important insight to the environmental factors that affect community decisions on whether to move or settle in an area. This data, in turn, can help inform effective community responses to contemporary climatological shifts. I will study environmental links to settlement patterns via a remotely sensed archaeological dataset and marine paleoclimate record. Three major aims will be addressed: 1. To what degree was resource distribution correlated with settlement density in Late Holocene SW Madagascar? 2. Are erratic shifts in environmental conditions, inferred from paleoclimate proxies during the Late Holocene in Southwest Madagascar, accompanied by shifts in the distribution and density of settlements? And 3. To what degree do Allee effects account for settlement patterns? Using an automated remote sensing procedure employing Sentinel-2 satellite imagery and machine learning algorithms, I will systematically record archaeological deposits in the Velondriake Marine Protected Area in southwest Madagascar. I will then survey and excavate identified deposits to acquire temporal information (e.g., 14C dates, ceramics, etc.). Next, I will take cores from fossil corals within the study region and analyze the stable isotope and trace element compositions to reconstruct past environmental conditions. Finally, I will use a geographic downscaling procedure to derive local climate conditions from aggregate values acquired from fossil corals and other extant paleoclimate records. These data will be incorporated into spatial point pattern analyses of archaeological settlements in the study area to investigate the relationship between environmental conditions and settlement distributions.

This project will highlight internal mobility throughout the southwest of Madagascar via landscape scale analysis, providing important information pertaining to adaptive strategies for environmental change. This is significant as most prior research on Madagascar has focused on human arrival but not on post-arrival mobility patterns. Understanding adaptive strategies for environmental change is also vital today, as climate change is becoming increasingly erratic. The planned study will also demonstrate how archaeologists can combine remote sensing datasets with spatial statistics and paleoclimatology to iteratively investigate the archaeological record. The methods proposed here will also assist local communities in conserving their cultural heritage by systematically recording human presence on the landscape through time. Future research in African archaeology, environmental science, and paleoecology research focusing on climate and environmental conditions in the Indian Ocean region will benefit from the data on paleoclimate and human responses to change generated by this study.
